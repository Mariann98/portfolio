Design for Emotion

The project explores the significance of emotion in creating an engaging user experience through creative research and explorative design. Throughout 1 period my team of 4 did several emotional explorations and an emotional installation which helped us create a design that elicits chosen emotion and which results in something good.

Problem: Eliciting anxiety through design

Anxiety is something a lot of people deal with every day but how to elicit it through design in order to benefit the user in the long term.

Solution: Alarm clock with a punishment system

For people with discipline and early waking up problems, our team offers the Alarmio app, which helps to wake up quickly and effectively by using the punishment system so that the users have strong and constant motivation to get up.

Step 1 Emotional Situation

The emotional situation we chose is "unexpected public performance". We came up with it by brainstorming different situations we have been through that made us very emotional. After diverging, we started combining the same situations in clusters and converged them by removing situations that were not relevant to everyone. This was followed up with dot voting which we did twice to come up with the final emotional situation.

Step 2 Emotional explorations

My team chose three emotional explorations which were: Mapping, Soundscape, and Box. For each exploration, we had a clear intention which we followed according to our execution plan in order to come up with findings and reflect on them.

The Mapping

We started this emotional exploration with the intent to dive deeper into the experience of unexpected public speaking and understand the underlying emotions that one feels before, during, and after the performance. Another goal of the personal emotional exploration was for us to get familiar with emotional granularity, and analyze our thoughts, feelings, actions, and bodily responses during the stages of the experience.On this graph you can see my situation.

The Soundscape

After the analysis of the emotional mapping, our team decided to focus on one specific part of the experience of unexpected public speaking for further exploration. In fact, the most curious part of the emotional mapping is the “during the performance” part. We discovered our main findings and differences in this particular part and agreed that the contrast in emotions is what makes it interesting to explore.

The Box

We started our third emotional exploration with the intent to see how people would react in two different situations according to their surroundings. With this exploration, we also want to show everyone what the environment can do to a person's mind depending on what it's reflecting. To make this possible, we will use a black box that is empty which will implement the stage fright - there is nothing to worry about but people are still afraid of public performance. The first part of our experiment should make the person feel exposed and anxious and in the second part, more confident and prepared.

Step 3 Exploration Insights & Design Principles before

After our explorations, we came up with the most important insights and created design principles for our emotional installation.

Insights
  1. Standing on a stage in front of a group of people causes a physical reaction like trembling and sweating that leads to insecurity and lost concentration.
  2. The bigger the stage the bigger the anxiety because empty space gives the feeling of having no option to hide and save your life in case of attack.
  3. Cheering after the performance can make the person feel rewarded and more confident in themselves.
Principles
  1. Physical discomfort occurs when the person is positioned in the middle of a spacious place (white, negative space).
  2. The anxiety rises when the place is dark and the only light spot is directed at the person.
  3. Concentration on the physical response (trembling, sweating) leads to its amplification.
  4. Positive feedback, in the end, makes the user perceive the whole experience as more positive.
  5. Cheerful sounds increase self-confidence and give a sense of recognition.

Step 4 Emotional Installation

We started our installation with the intent to see how people would react in the same situation but under different circumstances. Each visitor will stand on the chair that imitates the stage, have headphones on while watching the same video projected on the wall, and have their hands sprayed with water imitating sweat. The beginning and the end are the same, so the experience will start with the heart heavily beating and ends with applause from the audience. Half of the visitors will listen to a happy song with some enthusiastic cheering, the other half will continue listening to the heartbeat along with some anxious music.

Step 5 Exploration Insights & Design Principles after

After the emotional installation, we came up with the most important insights and created design principles for our iterations.

Insights
  1. The emotions of fear, horror and depression lengthen the subjective perception of time, while the emotions of joy and carelessness have almost no effect on the perception of time.
  2. The emotion translated through the sound manipulates our focus of attention and interpretation of what we see.
  3. Getting appraised after a bad performance confuses people and makes them feel not worthy of the award and the approval of the public.
  4. Listening to a positive soundscape makes people more physically active.
Principles
  1. The elevated surface triggers feelings of insecurity and stress (the chair in our case).
  2. The tense soundscape evokes the feeling of insecurity.
  3. A tense soundscape makes the participants interpret the situation through negative perceptions.
  4. Dark and heavy musical accompaniment influences the interpretation of observed events as negative.
  5. Positive sound elicits a rise of energy in the body.

Step 6 Ideation

We asked ourselves the question “How can we use anxiety to trigger something good?” and started brain dumping. Every team member did this individually. The next step was to vote on each other’s ideas and give suggestions for improvement. After that, we came together to cluster them and find our favorites. We did this through a discussion explaining our preferences and reasons for them. Throughout the process we found ourselves mixing guilt and anxiety so we chose the idea that seemed to be the closest to anxiety. We continued developing the alarm idea by making a few HMW questions which helped us clarify the idea. After three rounds of voting on each HMW, we decided on key features that we want to implement in our iteration.

Step 7 Iteration 1 and 2

First iteration

During our iteration, we decided to test the punishment system of the alarm clock. We agreed that the strongest ideas are charging money and auto-sending random photos from the user’s gallery to random contacts when the user snoozes or ignores the alarm. We intend to learn what the users do, think, and say about these features. We will define which punishment system causes more anxiety and focus on that in our design.

Execution

We have two different ideas to test. Each of us will test the features on one or two people. Our target group will be people with discipline problems, who experience real problems waking up in the morning, and who own an electric toothbrush. The first idea is to test the user by threatening to take their money away if they decide to snooze the alarm. We will implement that by calling the user in the early morning and notifying them that it’s time to wake up and brush their teeth. To be sure that the user woke we will ask to take a picture of them brushing their teeth, in case if they do not send a picture 5 minutes after the call they will be charged 5 euros. After this testing, we will interview the user to learn about their experience. The second idea is to test the user by threatening to upload a compromising picture of them if they decide to snooze the alarm. The implementation will be the same with the exception that if they do not send a picture within 5 minutes, we will post an embarrassing picture of them on Instagram.

Second iteration

For the second iteration we decided to test the modified idea with photo punishment. Our goal was to eliminate the blackmailing feeling of the photo punishment but still have it as a trigger to wake up quickly. So after a brainstorming session, we came up with the idea of photoshopped pictures of a person posted online if they snooze Alarmio. The photoshopped image should be embarrassing and awkward enough to motivate the person to wake up.

Execution

Each of us will test the new improved version of our alarm clock with one person. They will have the chance before the testing to take a look at the application to get a better understanding of what will happen. During the testing, we will call the user and threaten to upload a modified picture of them on Instagram if they decide to snooze their alarm. We will implement that by calling the user in the early morning and notifying them that it is time to wake up and brush their teeth. This time we won't end the call, but instead, let the tester hear a ticking sound and count down every minute to build up the anxiety. To be sure that the user woke up we will ask to take a picture of them brushing their teeth, in case they do not send a picture within 5 minutes, we will post a modified picture of them on Instagram. After this testing, we will interview the user to learn about their experience.

Step 8 Final emotional design

The main objective for our final design was to create an alarm clock application that will help the users to get up on time by triggering anxiety in them. The user needs to sign up for the app by connecting with Instagram and choosing 5 photos that the app is allowed to modify. Then the user needs to connect their electric toothbrush through Bluetooth which will help the application figure out whether the user is fully up or not. During the sign-up process, the user will be given a thorough introduction to how the app works.

In order to use Alarmio, the alarm needs to be set at the desired time of waking up. When it’s time to get up, the alarm goes off (anxiety triggering alarm sound) and if the user decides to indeed brush their teeth, the app will say “If you don’t brush your teeth within the next 5 minutes, a modified photo of you will be posted on Instagram.” The user will see a preview of the possible photo being posted, and the timer starts the countdown of 5 minutes making ticking noises. By turning on the toothbrush, Alarmio is able to detect that the process has started, and counts it finished when the user has been brushing their teeth for 2 minutes (the normal amount of time a person should brush their teeth).

When the task was successfully completed, Alarmio will congratulate the user and show them a streak in case the user has woken up several times without snoozing. But if the user decides to snooze, the application will say “Here is the last chance to redeem yourself. You have 1 minute to get up and brush your teeth or a modified photo of you will get posted on Instagram.” Alamrio shows a preview of the photo and starts the countdown and ticking sound. Then the process is the same if they choose to use their second chance. If they don’t, a modified photo will be posted on their Instagram which will be there for 24 hours without the option to take it down. A descriptive text will be added to the photo, to amplify the anxiety even more.

Skills acquired

  1. Demonstrating a basic understanding of emotions and their role in (UX) design.
  2. Planning and executing an in-depth exploration of an emotional situation, and translating these findings into valuable insights and Principles for design.
  3. Generating diversity of ideas, concepts, and prototypes (diverging) and evaluating these based on the exploration insights, Design Principles, testing, and feedback to make valuable iterations.
  4. Creating an emotional design that is a convincing answer to the design challenge and using video prototyping to showcase its workings and experience for a defined context and user(s) in a comprehensible way.
  5. Explaining one’s role and responsibility in the project and group dynamics and reflect on what one could personally improve in one’s contributions.
