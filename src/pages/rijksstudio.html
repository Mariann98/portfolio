Rijksstudio

During this project for 'Research for Design' course, my team of 4 analyzed the website of Rijksstudio. Our goal was to make the users’ experience better through research, which includes user trialing. For the success of this research, we had to figure out as a team the main usability problems by conducting user trialing and analyzing the results. The Rijksmuseum is a Dutch National museum dedicated to arts and history in Amsterdam. The Rijksstudio site is a platform developed by the Rijksmuseum to share art collections free of charge with the public in high quality that brings the Rijksmuseum to your home.

Problem: Reporting user's issues

Visitors of the Rijksstudio website face usability problems that need to be investigated.

Solution: Empirical research

Trialing the usability problems of the Rijksstudio website and proposing a design solution.

Step 1 Orientation and research questions

The user trial started by exploring the website as a team and noting the various features of the website. After that we created the site map and came up with the research questions.

  1. What do participants, who enjoy art, do when visiting the site: rijksstudio.nl?
  2. What usability problems are users facing when using rijksstudio.nl? What causes these problems?
  3. In what way are users incentivized to interact with the content and functionality of the studio website?
  4. How do users find information and art pieces on the studio website?

Step 2 Research set-up

During the trial, we used Zoom to capture the user's actions, sounds, and their facial expressions. Using Zoom was the most convenient way to do all three recordings at the same time. The user profile we chose was “young (18-30 years old) art enthusiast”. Those are the people who have a general interest in classical arts and visit museums at least twice a year. We chose this user group as we believe the Rijksmuseum is targeting them through their Studio webpage to inspire them with the available art and as Gen-Z/Millennials they are proactive users of online content. Each group member had to interview one participant and do the user-trialing with. This profile helped us find out what they, as art lovers actually do and find on the webpage, what kind of problems they might have and how they could plan their in-person visits to the museum.

Step 3 Data fieldwork

After the user trialling, each of us did a transcript of the interview and the video recording, which helped us create flowcharts in XD to have an overview of the users actions and emotions. Afterward we used the no creativity method to get an overview of the usability problems the participants faced.

1. What do participants, who enjoy art, do when visiting the site: rijksstudio.nl? Our first task for our participants was to explore it freely, so this really gives an overview of how they use the website on their own. The participants visited the gift shop, clicked on “Watch our stories” and “What’s on”. Checked out exhibitions and even watched a video.

2. What usability problems are users facing when using rijksstudio.nl? What causes these problems? During the trialling we noticed several usability problems which the user's were facing. The different functions as the route option or adding different art works to a set are not clearly communicated towards the user. The route option can only be seen below some sets and randomly appears among the artworks. Since the user doesn’t know about the set option and tries to add something to it, it gets confusing for the user where to add it exactly. The user was struggling with finding the correct search engine several times, which made it difficult to find the specific artworks on task 4.

3. In what way are users incentivized to interact with the content and functionality of the studio website? The visitors are incentivized with the website by viewing, creating, and sharing custom galleries of artworks put together by other users, picked from the large catalog. They can create an account, add artworks in their personal gallery, view others’ galleries, or view online tours and galleries made by the people from the museum. They have the option to ‘like’ the artwork, download a png, share it, and cycle through the gallery. Also, there are engaging headers that invite the users to “Watch our stories” or “Shop the collection”.

4. How do users find information and art pieces on the studio website? In case of finding information or art pieces, the user tends to use the Rijksmuseum main search engine instead of “Search in Rijksstudio”. This causes confusion for the visitor or Rijksstudio due to unnecessary information that occurs by using it. For example the user’s were having trouble finding specific artworks for task 4, due to using the main search engine. This made the process a lot longer.

Step 4 Results and conclusion

We recommend the Studio website to be an independent website with its own menu and functions from the Rijksmuseum website. With the new menu of the website the key functions would be clearly communicated towards the users, which would help them to use the website to its fullest potential.

Most of our users used the main website’s search engine to find functions or artworks. We recommend that only one search option should be on the same landing page and that option should search on the studio website’s art works and functions.

After creating the first set, our users didn’t know where to look for it. They tried the main menu, but it didn’t help. Some hints while creating the artset about where to find it or a button such as ‘look at the artset’ after the creation should help.

Navigating back to the home page of the Studio website is a long process, since the users can only go back page by page. If there would be a home button for the Studio website too users could find their way easier.

Some links looked like buttons that were used as links in other areas on the website. This created confusion for users when they tried to click on them. If there was more consistency across the website in how links are designed, then it would create less confusion for the user when looking where to click.

Finding the available routes option can only be seen below some sets or shows up randomly among artworks and sets. We recommend having a highlight for these sets on the top of the website as a result users will find it easily and start to plan their journey to the museum.

There are many options in the refine menu which creates a lot of information to absorb for the user. A recommendation would be to select your refinements before you search again as the page reloads after every refinement filter. It would also make it more efficient to have a ‘remove all’ function to remove the refinements as this is a one-at-a-time action.

  2. Planning and executing an in-depth exploration of an emotional situation, and translating these findings into valuable insights and Principles for design.

  4. Creating an emotional design that is a convincing answer to the design challenge and using video prototyping to showcase its workings and experience for a defined context and user(s) in a comprehensible way.
